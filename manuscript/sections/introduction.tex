\section{Motivation: scaffolding conformational epitopes}\label{sec:intro}

\subsection{Why scaffold epitopes}
Antibodies recognize their antigens through epitopes defined by three-dimensional geometry.
Many of the most interesting epitopes are discontinuous in sequence but contiguous in
space: the residues an antibody contacts are scattered along the antigen sequence yet fold
together into a single surface patch. This is precisely the kind of target that linear
methods cannot represent. High-throughput serology platforms such as PepSeq tile an antigen
into short, overlapping linear peptides, which works well for continuous, sequence-local
epitopes. A linear peptide, however, cannot reconstitute a surface assembled from
sequence-distant residues, and it discards the native conformation the antibody evolved to
recognize, so conformational and discontinuous epitopes fall through the gaps of linear
tiling.

The aim of this project (``episcaf'') is to close that gap. We take an antibody epitope
defined by its native three-dimensional geometry and build a folded, assay-ready protein
that displays the epitope the way it sits on the antigen. The longer-term goal is to extend
PepSeq-style high-throughput assays beyond linear peptides to conformational epitopes.

\subsection{The approach}
We extract the epitope, meaning the residues an antibody contacts, from an antibody:antigen
crystal structure. We embed those residues into a \emph{de novo} protein scaffold with
RFdiffusion, assign a sequence with ProteinMPNN, and then ask AlphaFold3 whether the design
actually folds with the epitope held in its native geometry. Designs are kept or rejected
on AlphaFold3 confidence and geometry-preservation metrics (Section~\ref{sec:methods}). The
full production pipeline, including the Nextflow orchestration, the RFdiffusion runs, and
the AlphaFold3 evaluation, originates in Lawson Woods' \texttt{episcaf-experiments}
repository. Reproducing and then extending it is the starting point of this work.

\subsection{Patches and islands}
A single antibody:antigen complex can yield more than one epitope \emph{patch}, labeled
\texttt{0P}, \texttt{1P}, and so on, where each patch is a distinct cluster of
antibody-contacting residues drawn from the same structure. A patch is in turn made of one
or more \emph{islands}, the spatially contiguous runs of epitope residues in
three-dimensional space. This distinction matters because it predicts difficulty.
Single-island patches can be enforced locally and tend to scaffold well, whereas
multi-island patches require preserving the relative position and orientation of spatially
separated regions, which is much harder.

A concrete failure makes the point. PDB \texttt{2XQB}, patch \texttt{0P}, is a 56-residue
epitope split across two separated islands. Across 2{,}624 RFdiffusion trials, zero designs
passed the downstream structural filters. Because this $0\%$ success rate persists over
thousands of attempts, it reads as a robust computational failure rather than
undersampling. The cause appears procedural: contigs (the constraints RFdiffusion is given)
enforce epitope residue identity and ordering, but they do not strongly constrain the
distances and orientations \emph{between} islands, so the model can satisfy the contig and
still lose the geometry an antibody needs. Single-island, compact patches run through the
identical pipeline and filters do produce passing designs, and the
\texttt{designFilterRates} summary shows success rates that correlate strongly with epitope
topology, particularly the number of islands.

\subsection{Two regimes: with and without a known antibody}
The project runs in two settings that differ in what ``correct'' can be measured against.
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Known-antibody (the ``DP3'' set).} When the antigen has a solved antibody
  complex, we have a ground truth: a design is good if its epitope is held in place and the
  scaffold does not collide with where the antibody must bind. This gives the four-filter
  pass definition used throughout (Section~\ref{sec:methods}).
  \item \textbf{No-antibody (the tiled 12-mer set).} Most targets have no solved antibody,
  so there is nothing to clash against. Here we tile the antigen into 12-residue epitopes
  and replace the antibody-clash filter with a native-antigen-aware accessibility surrogate
  (Section~\ref{sec:methods}) that asks whether scaffold mass intrudes into the volume an
  approaching antibody would need.
\end{itemize}
A single, config-driven composite scorer ranks designs in both regimes, differing only in
which accessibility term it uses.

\subsection{How the rest of this log reads}
The sections that follow are in the order the work happened. We start by reproducing
Lawson's workflow and confirming that our reimplementation of the metrics matches his
(Section~\ref{sec:methods}). We then turn to the filtering, where the original global
metrics bias selection toward rigid, well-ordered folds, and show how decomposing them into
epitope- and scaffold-specific terms widens the designable space
(Section~\ref{sec:filtering}). From there we build a composite score that selects the best
designs per epitope (Section~\ref{sec:composite}), and finally extend everything to
antigens with no known antibody using the native-aware cylinder surrogate
(Section~\ref{sec:noab}). Each step motivates the next.
