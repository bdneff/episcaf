\section{RFD1 versus RFD3 on the same epitopes}\label{sec:rfd}
With the metrics reproduced, we compared the two backbone generators head to head: the same
DP3 epitopes run through RFdiffusion1 (Lawson's set) and RFdiffusion3, each followed by
ProteinMPNN and AlphaFold3, and scored against the identical four filters. The RFD1 data here
is Lawson's deposited set (\texttt{dp2.parquet}), which is missing AlphaFold3 results for
\num{26870} of its \num{151232} designs; that is a property of his dataset rather than of our
pipeline, so we compare over designs that actually have an AF3 result. On that basis the two
are comparable, with RFD1 modestly higher: RFD1+MPNN passes \num{840}/\num{124362}
($0.68\%$) and RFD3+MPNN \num{751}/\num{150936} ($0.50\%$). RFD3 is therefore at least an
even trade for RFD1 on this task, which is the basis for standardizing on RFD3 going forward.
The per-metric distributions are broadly comparable (Fig.~\ref{fig:rfd}), with RFD3 placing
somewhat more mass at low epitope and overall RMSD.

It helps to frame what is actually being compared. Each protocol samples sequences from a
backbone-conditioned distribution $p(s\mid b)$ and is judged by the same pass rate
(Eq.~\ref{eq:passrate}), so a difference in $\hat P_{\text{pass}}$ reflects a difference in
those sequence distributions over comparable backbones. ProteinMPNN optimizes $p(s\mid b)$
directly through its inverse-folding objective, whereas RFD3 produces it only as a by-product
of all-atom denoising; that the two land so close is the substantive result.

The aggregate pass counts hide a more interesting difference in \emph{where} the passes
land (Fig.~\ref{fig:passep}). RFD1's passes are highly concentrated: a single target
(\texttt{7ox3\_0P}) accounts for \num{630} of its \num{840} passes, and just two targets
account for the large majority. RFD3 spreads its \num{751} passes across more targets (16
with at least one pass, versus 10 for RFD1), and its per-target leaders are different
(\texttt{4xwo\_5P}, \texttt{8pww\_0P}, and \texttt{8db4\_0P} all pass well under RFD3 but
barely or not at all under RFD1). For a library meant to cover many epitopes, that broader
coverage is arguably more useful than a similar total concentrated on one target.
